ONE_LINER: MWX is a plug-and-play AI tools marketplace for SMEs on Base, combining a Web2-style UX with MWXT token mechanics for payments, staking, discounts and governance.

===============================================================================
1) CORE VALUE PROP
===============================================================================
- Problem: SMEs want AI automation across marketing, finance, ops and HR but often can’t afford or integrate heavy “enterprise AI” solutions.
- Solution: MWX packages AI tools as ready-to-use modules and offers them via a marketplace UI, so SMEs can subscribe or purchase without deep technical integration.
- Differentiation: Hybrid model—Web2-style UX and fiat-friendly onboarding paired with Web3 settlement (on Base) and a token economy that ties marketplace activity to MWXT demand and incentives.
- Why now: Aligns with agentic AI and AI-SaaS narratives, but targeted at a concrete segment (SMEs) and backed by transparency primitives (burn logs, purchase counters) rather than pure meme dynamics.

4) MAIN RISKS
===============================================================================
- Smart contract and upgradeability risk:
  - Token and related contracts are upgradeable; this allows fixes and iteration, but introduces admin-key and governance-risk surface area.
  - Explorer UIs may show “no audit submitted,” even where project materials mention audits—scope and coverage should be independently verified.
- Token/economics and emissions risk:
  - 1B fixed supply with structured allocation (team, DAO, ecosystem, public/private, advisors, liquidity, etc.) and lock/vesting schedules creates predictable unlock events and concentration risks.
  - Staking models (flexible vs locked, lock multipliers, emergency-unstake with forfeits) can attract mercenary capital; sustainability depends on whether rewards are revenue-backed or mostly emissions.
- Business and PMF risk:
  - SME distribution is hard work: onboarding, localisation, support and enterprise trust-building vary by region; the project emphasises SEA/SME focus, which is coherent but execution-heavy.
  - Competitive landscape includes both Web2 AI SaaS suites and other AI/agent marketplace projects; MWX’s edge must come from real SME usage and third-party vendor supply.
- Centralisation, custody and regulatory risk:
  - Fiat acceptance and off-chain payment handling imply payment processors and compliance/ops points where disputes and regulations apply.
  - Governance is currently more centralised, with DAO-style governance presented as a mid‑to‑long‑term goal (e.g. 2028) rather than a fully live system.
  - Legal analysis docs frame MWXT as a utility token, not equity—but such opinions are jurisdiction-specific and not blanket regulatory approvals.

5) NATIVE DAPPS (THE “CORE” APP LAYER)
============================================================
A) Asset Passport
Purpose
- A “digital identity” for each tokenized property/asset, aggregating attested ownership history, legal docs, valuations, and maintenance records from trusted sources.
Design notes
- Hybrid data model: immutable on-chain records for legally recognized status + encrypted off-chain repositories for detailed documents/inspections/images/filings.
- Intended cross-chain synchronization of signed updates to maintain consistent asset views across chains.
- Planned to be integrated into the Integra block explorer (per docs).

B) Global Orderbook
Purpose
- A back-end orderbook for real estate tokens to enable price discovery, liquidity pooling, and secondary market trading.
Design notes
- Connects siloed venues via shared listing/matching fabric.
- Supports intermediaries (e.g., licensed brokers) and embeds commissions/commission payouts into lifecycle.
- Vision includes: central repository (asset data, investor eligibility, commission terms) + distribution network (instant venue connectivity) + “ecosystem flywheel” to avoid fragmentation.

C) Native Stablecoin
Purpose
- Primary unit of account and settlement asset on the chain.
Design notes
- Issued by a regulated entity, redeemable at par.
- 1:1 backed by “risk-free assets” (as claimed).
- Returns from backing assets are described as being invested in real estate (per docs); also “revenue capture” is mentioned (details not fully specified on the snippet page).

D) Compliance & Identity Layer (as a “native dApp/module”)
Purpose
- Turn regulations into machine-readable policies enforced in transactions.
Design notes
- Uses W3C DIDs for participant identifiers.
- Uses VCs from regulated issuers (KYC/KYB, accreditation, sanctions status, license type, etc.).
- Policies can encode eligibility, transfer restrictions, lock-ups, jurisdiction constraints, travel-rule/reporting obligations.
- Enforced as gating logic: only execute state transitions when policies satisfied.

E) Token Issuance Framework
Purpose
- Standardize issuance patterns / reference contracts for tokenized real estate and related instruments.
Design notes
- Issuers define an issuance profile: legal wrapper (SPV/fund/trust/etc.), jurisdiction/regime, investor eligibility, rights, transfer restrictions, redemption rules.
- Framework instantiates audited reference contracts integrating compliance/identity + asset passport.
- Supports on-chain, hybrid, and intermediary-coordinated institutional flows.
- Lifecycle management includes: updating disclosures, corporate actions, distributions, redemptions, wind-downs; recorded to the Asset Passport.

12) DUE DILIGENCE CHECKLIST (WHAT TO VERIFY BEFORE COMMITTING)
============================================================
A) Tech & network
- Is there a public testnet? Explorer? RPC endpoints? Chain ID? EVM compatibility in practice?
- Is there developer documentation beyond the high-level GitBook?
- Validator set: who are the initial validators (names, operators, stake distribution)?

B) Compliance & legal
- Who issues the stablecoin? Under what jurisdictional regime? What reserves, which banks/custodians?
- Who issues verifiable credentials? Which KYC/KYB providers are integrated?
- How are transfer restrictions enforced for different jurisdictions?

C) Real-world adoption
- Any named pilot programs with asset managers/developers?
- Evidence of assets tokenized: sample “asset passports,” issuance data, secondary trades, broker integrations.

D) Token economics
- Vesting schedules, lockups, emission schedules, burn policy specifics.
- Treasury transparency: wallets, governance controls, reporting.

E) Risks
- Regulatory risk (real estate is jurisdiction-heavy).
- Stablecoin risk (issuer/reserves/banking).
- Market structure risk (global orderbook needs many venues & brokers to work).
- Execution risk (moving from narrative to production infra is hard).
